Instructions

Welcome! In this task, we're dissecting rules-of-thumb (ROTs) about social norms that other people have written.

This task has many components, so the instructions are written in several parts. I know it's a lot to remember, so I've included each part of the instructions in a the main task as well. Wherever you see ► Help in the main task, you can click it to check out that question's guide and examples.

What portion of people probably agree?

This question is about determining how universally held a belief is. Please answer this about people in the United States.

We have five buckets you can choose from. Here are some examples of each bucket.

  • < 1% (Almost no one)
    • Murdering people is great
    • You should never leave your home
  • ~ 5% – 25% (People occasionally think this)
    • Ghosts exist
    • It's wrong to seek help
  • ~ 50% (Controversial)
    • It's okay to have an abortion
    • It's important to reduce our carbon footprint of climate change
    • It's good to own weapons to protect your family
  • ~ 75% – 90% (Common belief)
    • It's okay to try again when you fail
    • Seeking help when you need it is a good thing
  • > 99% (Universally true)
    • It's good to take a feverish baby to the doctor
    • It's bad to deliberately hurt your pets

ROT Categorization

Here we want to categorize ROTs according to the type of information they provide. There are four (4) possible categories to choose from. Since many ROTs can fall into more than one of the categories, please pick up to two (2) categories for the ROT.

We prefer you pick the fewest number of categories. That means pick one (1) when possible, though you will often need to pick two (2) categories. You should rarely need three (3).

  • Morality / Ethics
    • The ROT evokes rules or guiding principles that deal with the concepts of right or wrong conduct.
    • Examples:
      • It's good to help the needy.
      • It's wrong to turn your back on your friends who need your help.
      • Telling a secret you have promised to keep is bad.
  • Social Norms
    • The ROT deals with standards of appropriate behavior in a society. These norms emphasize social conventions, or expectations of what is considered an appropriate conduct. They allow for harmonious co-existence in society.
    • Examples:
      • Being obnoxious and loud in public is bad.
      • It’s good to shake hands with your opponent even if you lost.
      • It’s not acceptable to block an grocery aisle so that you can talk on the phone.
  • Advice
    • The ROT deals with helpful (or unhelpful) advice concerning prudent behaviors that are aimed at improving one’s life.
    • Examples:
      • It’s good to eat when you are hungry.
      • It’s good to take medicine your doctor prescribes
      • Grudges can hurt relationships.
  • It is what it is
    • The ROT does not provide advice or deal with questions of ethics, morality or social norms. It simply describes how things are.
    • Examples:
      • Sometimes people can get stressed.
      • Bananas are for eating.
      • It is nice to be tall.

Moral Foundations

Moral foundations categories are cultural values or concerns that promote cooperation. Each virtue is paired up with its opposing vice. Select categories that best fit the ROTs.

Again, we ask you to pick the best fit, rather than marking all that might apply. We prefer you pick the one (1) best foundation, or sometimes the two (2) best, rather than checking many of them for every conceivable angle. Or, sometimes you won’t check any (e.g., “for “Bananas are for eating” or “It’s nice to be tall.”).

  • Care / Harm
    • Deals with the ability to feel the pain or suffering of self and others.
    • Examples:
      • Safety issues should be addressed.
      • It’s okay to tell others how you feel.
  • Fairness / Cheating
    • Deals with notions of equity, justice, and rights.
    • Examples:
      • You should respect your partner’s choices.
      • It’s rude not to tip.
  • Loyalty / Betrayal
    • Deals with obligations or concerns for group, family, and nation.
    • Examples:
      • It’s expected that you respect your parents.
      • It’s okay to outgrow your old friends.
  • Authority / Subversion
    • Deals with submission and deference to traditions or legitimate authority.
    • Examples:
      • Always drive as if there is a police officer somewhere.
      • It’s expected that you obey your parents.
  • Sanctity / Degradation
    • Deals with abhorrence for disgusting things, foods, or actions.
    • Examples:
      • It’s okay to be disgusted by vomit.
      • It’s good not to waste food.
      • It’s OK to choose who you have sexual relations with.

Who is the ROT targeting?

Which of the characters in the sentence is the ROT about? Whose behavior is being analyzed in terms of rules, regulations, or social norms?

  • Situation: This urged him to drop his soda addiction.
    • ROT: People will think you have a strong will if you break an addiction.
    • Character: "him" is the focus of the ROT
  • Situation: Suspicion, Deception Surround Elderly Father's Younger Wife
    • ROT: Children shouldn’t have to marry so young
    • Character: The "Younger Wife" is the focus of the ROT
  • Situation: Calling the cops on my neighbor because of his cat
    • ROT: It’s rude to call the cops on someone you know
    • Character: The "Narrator" is the one who called the cops, so they are the focus of this ROT

If the target of the ROT isn’t identifiable, or is a non-narrator entity that is not mentioned in the sentence, simply pick “no one listed” (this should hopefully be rare).

Split ROT Into: Actions & Judgments

ROTs are generally composed of an ACTION and a JUDGMENT about the action. Here, we are asking you to split the two components. They wording does not have to be word for word, as you can see in the following examples.

  • ROT: It's good to help elderly people
    • Action: helping elderly people
    • Original judgment: it's good
  • ROT: The holidays are expected to be especially difficult for those that are grieving.
    • Action: enjoying the holidays while grieving
    • Original judgment: is difficult
  • ROT: People will think you have a strong will if you break an addiction.
    • Action: breaking an addiction
    • Original judgment: takes a strong will
  • ROT: It's understandable that sometimes, things happen and people don't have family members to learn from.
    • Action: not having family members to learn from
    • Original judgment: it’s understandable
  • ROT: If you think someone is in danger you are expected to call the police
    • Action: calling the police when you think someone is in danger
    • Original judgment: it’s expected
  • ROT: Children shouldn’t have marry so young
    • Action: children marrying very young
    • Original judgment: should not happen
  • ROT: Making a truce with a rival can lead to less stress in your life.
    • Action: making a truce with a rival
    • Original judgment: can lead to less stress in your life
  • ROT: You can't expect the people in your life to always give you the best of everything.
    • Action: expecting the people in your life to always give you the best of everything
    • Original judgment: you can’t
  • ROT: It's okay to be angry if your friend talks to someone you used to date.
    • Action: being angry when your friend talks to someone you used to date
    • Original judgment: it's okay

Agency vs Experience?

This question is trying to figure out whether the action is something you can do (agency), or whether it’s something that just happens (experience).

We ask this because the next questions are about how good/bad it is to do the action, and how much pressure you feel do the action. Those questions only make sense for actions that can be done by choice—i.e., actions marked with agency.

Here are some examples of both:

  • Agency
    • Doing the dishes
    • Calling the police
    • Being considerate
    • Expecting something from someone
  • Experience
    • Someone's spouse dying
    • Your spouse dying
    • Being stuck in an earthquake

There are also some difficult-to-decide cases. When in doubt, think about whether you could have any choice in the action.

  • Experience Feeling upset when someone calls you a name
  • Agency Being mad for two days when someone calls you a name
  • Agency Taking revenge on someone for calling you a name

Often, the ROTs may not have enough information for you to make a clear call. For example, the action might just be “being mad.” In these cases, make your best call, taking into account clues from the situation and whether the next questions make sense.

Action Judgment

This question has to do with your judgment of what is good and bad when it comes to a behavior or action.

What we mean by "good" and "bad" are morality / ethics / social norms.

Try to transcribe the original judgment. But, if the original judgment is obviously wrong, pick the correct bucket instead. For example, if someone wrote "It's good to eat bananas" (this is a real ROT), we would say this is actually "Expected / OK" in terms of morality / ethics / social norms, not "Good"

Here are descriptions and examples of the different bins:

  • Very bad
    • Behaviors or actions that are spiteful, malicious or insulting. These behaviors can harm or injure others (or other people’s property).
    • Examples:
      • Destroying someone’s phone
      • Kicking a dog
      • Slashing tires for fun
  • Bad
    • Behaviors or actions that are rude or unkind. These behaviors affect others negatively.
    • Examples:
      • Littering
      • Saying something mean to a friend
      • Playing loud music in a bus (without earphones)
  • Expected / OK
    • Normal behaviors. Such behaviors are generally considered to be expected by the society around you. In other words, you might not get any "kudos" for doing these things, though you might experience repercussions if you do not!
    • Examples:
      • Wearing clothes
      • Showing up to work on time
      • Cleaning up when you make a mess
  • Good
    • Behaviors or actions that are kind or considerate, such as a nice gesture or small acts of kindness. These behaviors affect others positively.
    • Examples:
      • Driving a friend to the airport
      • Complimenting your colleague on work well done
      • Making a dinner for a sick friend
  • Very good
    • Behaviors or actions that are generous, self-sacrificing, or perhaps even heroic. These behaviors impact others in significant ways.
    • Examples:
      • Taking some time or money to help someone in need
      • Volunteering at a hospital in your spare hours
      • Buying groceries for financially struggling neighbor

Legality

Here, you are being asked to say whether the ACTION abides by law where you live (i.e., your particular state in the U.S.). You can choose one of three choices:

  • Illegal
    • The action is clearly and definitely illegal. You will be held accountable and prosecuted by law if you go through this action.
    • Examples:
      • Blackmailing
      • Committing arson
      • Abusing a child
      • Defrauding Medicaid
  • Depends / tolerated
    • The action generally falls in “illegal” category, but depending on the nature, circumstances, or location (e.g. State, City) of the commission, it might be unenforced or tolerated.
    • Examples:
      • Using cellphone while driving
      • Connecting to neighbor’s unsecured Wi-Fi
      • Playing poker at home
      • Speeding
  • Legal
    • This action is not illegal.
    • Examples:
      • Coughing without covering one’s mouth
      • Eating noisily
      • Drinking soda with lunch
      • Sleeping

Cultural Pressure

Culture pressure gauges the level of expectation that is associated to do or not do an action. The expectation is generally imposed by society or the culture that surrounds us.

Cultural pressure could come from your family, friends, community, culture, or society at large.

The following examples are guidelines only. Please evaluate according to your own feelings.

  • Strong pressure against
    • Current culture frowns upon this action. You feel strongly pressured NOT TO DO the action.
    • Examples:
      • Walking around naked in public
      • Intentionally harming an animal
  • Pressure against
    • Current culture generally discourages this action. You feel some or moderate pressure NOT TO DO this action.
    • Examples:
      • Spending money on jewelry if you can’t afford it.
      • Being loud in public places
  • Discretionary
    • Current culture has nothing to say about this action. You feel little or no pressure to do, or not do, the action.
    • Examples:
      • Eating chips while relaxing
      • Choosing not to read before bed
      • Liking sports (true for some people; others may feel some pressure on one direction!)
  • Pressure for
    • Current culture generally encourages this action. You feel some or moderate pressure to do this action.
    • Examples:
      • Being nice when posting online
      • Being honest with people
  • Strong pressure for
    • Current culture strongly promotes this action. You feel strong pressure to DO this action.
    • Examples:
      • Wearing clothes in public
      • Not cheating on your spouse
      • Helping your children when they are ill

Who is (or might be) doing the action?

Here we simply want to identify whether anyone in the situation is—or might be—doing the action. Here are some examples:

  • Situation: She left India knowing her true calling in life.
    • ROT: You should follow your dreams
    • Action: following your dreams
    • Character: "She"

Sometimes, the character isn’t explicitly doing the action, but might be encouraging or promoting it. We still mark the most relevant person, but will mark it as “probable” in the next question.

  • Situation: His dad said you need to beat up anyone who gets in your way
    • ROT: It’s wrong to physically hurt people
    • Action: physically hurting people
    • Character: "His dad"

Sometimes the action would only happen hypothetically. We still mark the person who might be doing the action, but we mark that this is “hypothetical” in the next step. For example:

  • Situation: Jamie sneezed loudly
    • ROT: It’s polite to cover your mouth when you sneeze.
    • Action: covering your mouth when you sneeze
    • Character: "Jamie"

The thing to be careful for is that usually, only one of the action or the reverse action has someone doing it. So, to extend the previous example:

  • Reverse action: not covering your mouth when you sneeze
    • Both the action and the reverse action are hypothetical for Jamie. In this case, simply pick the action (not the reverse), and say “no one listed” as the character for the reverse action.

Hypothetical, Probable, or Explicit?

Above, we picked who is (or may be) doing the action. This question is about whether they were definitely (explicitly) doing it, whether they probably were, or whether the action (and the ROT) was about something hypothetical that could happen.

Here's an example of each kind:

  • Situation: Fighting with my younger brother over computer time
    • ROT: It's good to apologize to someone after you fight
    • Action: apologizing to someone after you fight
      • Character: Narrator or younger brother
      • Hypothetical
      • We have no evidence either way whether this happened
    • ROT: It's bad to yell at others
    • Action: yelling at others
      • Character: Narrator or younger brother
      • Probable
      • We’re not totally sure that there was yelling, but it seems likely given two siblings were fighting
    • ROT: It's expected that sometimes you fight with your siblings
    • Action: fighting with your siblings
      • Character: Narrator or younger brother
      • Explicit
      • This definitely happened

Writing the Reverse Action

For the reverse action, write the action opposite to the action you wrote earlier. Usually, you can simply negate the action with "not."

  • ROT: It's good to care for animals
    • Action: caring for animals
    • Reverse action: not caring for animals
  • ROT: If you think someone is in danger you are expected to call the police
    • Action: calling the police when you think someone is in danger
    • Reverse action: not calling the police when you think someone is in danger

In some cases, there may be more than one reverse action available for an action depending on the nature or intensity of the action. In such cases, simply choose one.

  • ROT: People will think you have a strong will if you break an addiction.
    • Action: breaking an addiction
    • Reverse action 1: not breaking out of an addiction
    • Reverse action 2: continuing an addiction
  • ROT: The holidays are expected to be especially difficult for those that are grieving.
    • Action: enjoying the holidays while grieving
    • Reverse action 1: not enjoying the holidays while grieving
    • Reverse action 2: hating the holidays while grieving
  • ROT: Children shouldn’t have to marry so young
    • Action: children marrying very young
    • Reverse action 1: children marrying not too young
    • Reverse action 2: children not marrying very young
    • Reverse action 3: children marrying once they’re older

Keeping the spirit of the ROT, what's the best judgment for ?

Help

Action Judgment

This question has to do with your judgment of what is good and bad when it comes to a behavior or action.

What we mean by "good" and "bad" are morality / ethics / social norms.

Try to transcribe the original judgment. But, if the original judgment is obviously wrong, pick the correct bucket instead. For example, if someone wrote "It's good to eat bananas" (this is a real ROT), we would say this is actually "Expected / OK" in terms of morality / ethics / social norms, not "Good"

Here are descriptions and examples of the different bins:

  • Very bad
    • Behaviors or actions that are spiteful, malicious or insulting. These behaviors can harm or injure others (or other people’s property).
    • Examples:
      • Destroying someone’s phone
      • Kicking a dog
      • Slashing tires for fun
  • Bad
    • Behaviors or actions that are rude or unkind. These behaviors affect others negatively.
    • Examples:
      • Littering
      • Saying something mean to a friend
      • Playing loud music in a bus (without earphones)
  • Expected / OK
    • Normal behaviors. Such behaviors are generally considered to be expected by the society around you. In other words, you might not get any "kudos" for doing these things, though you might experience repercussions if you do not!
    • Examples:
      • Wearing clothes
      • Showing up to work on time
      • Cleaning up when you make a mess
  • Good
    • Behaviors or actions that are kind or considerate, such as a nice gesture or small acts of kindness. These behaviors affect others positively.
    • Examples:
      • Driving a friend to the airport
      • Complimenting your colleague on work well done
      • Making a dinner for a sick friend
  • Very good
    • Behaviors or actions that are generous, self-sacrificing, or perhaps even heroic. These behaviors impact others in significant ways.
    • Examples:
      • Taking some time or money to help someone in need
      • Volunteering at a hospital in your spare hours
      • Buying groceries for financially struggling neighbor

What portion of people probably agree?

This question is about determining how universally held a belief is. Please answer this about people in the United States.

We have five buckets you can choose from. Here are some examples of each bucket.

    • It's bad to deliberately hurt your pets
