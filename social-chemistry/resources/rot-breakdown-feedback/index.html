  • Q: It seems like a lot of these don't have a clear person who is targeted? It seems like the narrator is giving advice to someone. For example, take the situation "Take Simple Steps to Avoid Delays at Doctor's Office," and the ROT "It's good to plan ahead to make sure you have everything you need when going to an appointment." (No one gives advice to Abby!) The ROT is (often) also targeting the person she’s giving advice to. So, all that to say, "no one listed” is the best answer for situations like that!

  • Q: What moral foundation do we pick for ROTs about having freedom to do what you want? For example, "you don't have to go to the movies," or "it's ok to have an opinion," or "it's ok to like rap music?"

    • Answer: These really fall under a sixth moral foundation: “liberty / oppression.”I should have included it, but I didn’t (facepalm). So, please don’t mark any moral foundations if you believe it falls into that category! Some workers have asked me about whether this would be “authority,” but I think that’s not quite a good fit here. I think of "authority" when there’s more clearly someone else with more/less power involved.

  • Q: I would love to have an example for (moral foundations for) a racism related ROT. For some reason these throw me off on whether racism is unfair, harmful, imposing authority over people, etc.

    • Answer: That's a great question. I think you could easily argue that it falls in all of those categories. You should mark all of those that come to mind for you. To be honest, most examples of moral foundations I've seen deal with pathologically simple examples, so they don't have a clear guideline for something as complex as racism. I'd argue you should mark all the ones you listed, especially because racism affects people differently, and brings potentially different moral issues to mind.

  • Q: I would also love an example of an ROT dealing with Sanctity (not degradation). I'm always unsure about this one. For example, would murder be violating the sanctity of life. Having a sanctity example might help clarify this a lot.

Another way of thinking about sanctity that might be more helpful is "purity." Your example about life fits. ROT Breakdowns: ROT Categorization Examples (Finally!)

Hi everyone,

(You're receiving this email because you're qualified to work on the ROT Breakdown project.)

I poured through a ton of your responses over the last couple batches. Great work!! We've been making incredible progress, the annotations are looking really good, and people are asking smart questions. Thank you so much for the effort you've been putting in, I really appreciate it!

I wanted to focus this message on helping out with ROT Categorization. From my end, I think folks have been doing really well on this overall. But I think many of you would like a bit more guidance and clarification to feel confident in your work. So let's see if I can help!

  • Morality (/ ethics) has to do with what's "right" or "wrong", even if the ROT doesn't use those words. These are somewhat rare. Here are some recent ROTs I'd mark as morality/ethics:

    • It's good to give people their lost items back
    • It's understandable to be concerned when your 12 year old injects a needle into their veins.
    • It is wrong to steal
    • It's not okay to let yourself be used for other's gain.
    • It's wrong to be greedy.

  • Social norms are about conventions we have. They often bleed into the "morality/ethics" and "advice" categories, so don't be afraid to mark both when that happens! Here are ROTs I'd mark as "social norms" (sometimes with another category):

    • If you drop something, you should pick it up.
    • It is reasonable to be upset if someone goes through your things.
    • It is expected people will take a lot of pictures at special events.
    • You should always support your friends when they have a new baby.
    • (+ advice) It is good to show appreciation when someone does something nice for you.
    • (+ advice) It's good to visit your partner's place.
    • (+ advice) It's good to be concerned with how you look.

  • Advice is often mixed with social norms. In fact, it can be hard to imagine advice without some idea of how society operates. My rule-of-thumb (ha, ha) is to add "social norms" when the advice has to do with behavior between multiple people, or society's perceptions. Examples of advice:

    • You should always buy your favorite foods if they are on sale.
    • You shouldn't trust cheaters.
    • It's good to be responsible for your animals.
    • (+ social norms) You should be independent.
    • (+ social norms) It is nice to be supportive when someone is making a positive life change.
    • (+ social norms) It's good to do things to make someone to feel special.

  • It is what it is ROTs are about the way things are. I usually mark this if the ROT describes just how things are, and none of the other categories seem to be at play. Sometimes you have to ignore the judgment (e.g., "it's good") part of the ROT, because it can be misleading.

    • It is annoying to feel powerless in a situation.
    • It's good to take part in Medieval Times since it's a ton of fun eating there.
    • It's good to adopt a new pet.
    • It's normal for people to take vacations.

That's it from me for now. I hope you enjoy the final ROT batch for today, and have a great Friday evening!

Max

Email message

Hi everyone,

I wanted to answer a couple questions I've gotten recently before posting the next batch!

Question: What do we do about vague ROTs that seem disconnected from the situation? For example, for the situation "I read the book you gave me," should we mark as bad the ROT "It is good to be yourself?"

Answer: As usual, I'd say the answer is "it depends!" Let me give two examples.

Sometimes, an ROT like "It is good to be yourself" is actually relevant, like:

  • Situation: "Following my passions despite what my boyfriend thinks"
    • ROT: "It's good to be yourself"
    • good!

... but on the other hand, sometimes the ROT is truly just a cop-out:

  • Situation: "I read the book you gave me"
    • ROT: "It's good to be yourself"
    • trash!

in those cases, it is OK to use the big button that marks the ROT as bad and move on!

I would say if the ROT is relevant at all, keep it, but if it's just totally generic, feel free to mark it as bad!

Question: What do we do about ROTs with "because" in them? For example "You shouldn't be broke, because then you can't pay your bills."

Answer: You can go either way on these. It's ideal if you can change the "because" into something that enriches the action. For example:

  • ROT: You shouldn't be broke, because then you can't pay your bills.
    • Action: being broke and unable to pay your bills

... but it's also fine to remove the "because" for ones where it's difficult to naturally combine the ideas:

  • ROT: Wanting to tie the knot, because life's short and brutal
    • Action: wanting to tie the knot

Question: What do we do about ROTs with two actions in them? For example, "It's important to care for yourself and not be too hard on yourself."

Answer: For the two-action ROTs, if you can identify one clear action, please do that and keep them!

  • ROT: It's important to care for yourself and not be too hard on yourself.

    • Action: taking care of yourself

  • ROT: It's expected that children, not adults, like watching cartoons.

    • Action: children liking to watch cartoons

Ideally, we would be able to identify both actions in two separate breakdowns. But that will have to be left for a future project! :-)

Thanks everyone!!

Max

Happy Thursday everyone!

Thank you for your hard work on the last few batches! More coming your way soon.

I appreciate you all writing in your thoughts and commentary, both email and in the feedback box. I'm still reviewing HITs giving individual feedback. Some more of you should have gotten individual feedback today. If you haven't yet, hopefully in a batch soon :-)

Here are a few more common things I spotted in reviewing HITs recently.

  1. For the ACTION, try to make sure it's phrased as something you can do. This usually means second person (you/your). For example, "looking at the clock" or "driving your car." So, for example, if we had an ROT like "parents paying attention to their children", we would NOT transcribe the action as "paying attention to their children". Instead, we have a couple main choices. One is "paying attention to your children" (second person). The other is "parents paying attention to their children" (third person). In general, I prefer the "your" as long as the action is super clear. (For this example, it's clear that "your children" implies you are a parent, so including "parent" is redundant. But other times, it does make sense to include the subject if it's not implied by the action.)

  2. Remember that "if" often turns into "when" for turning ROT into ACTION! E.g., for the ROT "It's good to use headphones if your music is loud", we would write the ACTION as "using headphones when your music is loud". This makes it more action-y.

  3. If you find yourself clicking the "> 99%" agreement option all of the time, consider if this is really totally universal! Often it is, so I don't want to make everyone second-guess themselves. But in the last round, I saw a handful of folks using that choice for somewhat controversial ideas. Really consider if everyone would agree on that idea, or if sometimes people might think otherwise.

That's all from me for now! Enjoy your day!

  • Question: Should I try to mark what I think other people will mark? The individual feedback has compared me to others' responses, and I'm worried about answering differently!

  • Question: How do I know if the narrator is the one doing the action? For example, take the situation, "not telling a friend she's getting fired from work." Is the narrator the one who has a friend, and is not telling her this?

    • Answer: Please assume the narrator is the one doing the situation! So for this example, we'd imagine there's a narrator who is telling us: "hey, here's this thing I'm doing: not telling a friend she's getting fired from work."

  • Question: How do we pick the judgment for "it is what it is" ROTs? For example, "People sometimes do things they shouldn't" or "People can get stressed out sometimes"?

    • Answer: Usually, "expected / OK" is the judgment for ROTs that are "it is what it is" statements. Some ROTs don't have a clear judgment written in them. Instead, some ROTs just say things that generally happen. In these cases, we'd probably pick the implicit judgment "expected / OK."

      For example, if an ROT says "People can get stressed out sometimes", you can think of that like someone saying "it's expected that people can get stressed out sometimes." So we'd say action = "people getting stressed out sometimes" and judgment = "it's expected". As for the graded judgment (where you pick a radio button), usually this is also "expected / OK".

      However, for some statements, you might pick a different choice. For example, in the ROT "People sometimes do things they shouldn't," we might say that this judgment is expected, but also slightly bad. So either choice is OK.

  • Question: A lot of the situations use the word "wanting", as in "not wanting to go to the store." For the last question (are they doing the action?) how should we be marking those, since they are saying they want or don't want to do something? Should we assume they actually did / didn't do it, or mark it as hypothetical / probable?

    • Answer: For "not wanting to do X," we definitely wouldn't say that X is "explicit" or "explicit no." We don't know whether they ended up doing the action or not. All we know is that they want (or don't want) to do it. So, we would choose from "hypothetical," "probable," or the new "probably not."

      What's the best choice among those? I think "hypothetical" is usually the best choice if all we know is that they want (or don't want) to do something. We don't know what actually happened, so we can just pick "hypothetical!"

Extended feedback

Here are my answers to the ten most common questions. Thank you again to everyone who wrote in with feedback and questions!

  • Question: What do I do about ROTs that don't have a judgment? There are many ROTs that seem like they can't be split into action and judgment. These often fall into the "it is what it is" category. For example, "Friends sometimes want to wear matching outfits."

    • Answer: For many "it is what it is" ROT, the judgment is implicitly "expected / OK." You can transcribe it like this: the action is "friends sometimes wearing matching outfits," and the moral judgment is "expected / OK." After all, the ROT is simply describing what people do sometimes.

      But sometimes there are ROTs that you really can't figure out how to break down. Maybe they're super unclear, or just plain incomprehensible. For these, please check the big dark box under the text entry that starts with "The ROT is confusing..." and submit the HIT! This box is there exactly so you can flag ROTs where it's too hard to split a clear action and judgment. (You'll need to simply make your best guesses for the top section.)

    • Change I made: I made the "Can't split the rot into action and judgment" button bigger and darker so it's more noticeable. I also added to it the text "ROT is confusing, extremely vague, or very low quality." Please feel free to use it whenever you feel like the ROT is a lost cause and should be trashed!

  • Question: What do I do about vague or confusing ROTs? Some ROTs are very hard to understand, even using the situation as context. Or maybe they're full of typos, or just plain crap. (Hopefully this is rare!)

    • Answer: Use the big dark button (starts with "The ROT is confusing...", right below the action/judgment text entry) to mark the ROT as garbage, and submit the HIT. (You'll still need to make your best guesses for the top section.) See the question above for more details.

      Many workers feel bad about using buttons like these. Obviously we don't want to abuse this button to skip work, and we want to make an effort to understand and break down ROTs whenever possible. But for truly bad ROTs, it really helps me when you tell me about them. I want to know about bad ROTs so I can discard them later.

  • Question: How should I rate actions that affect the doer of the action positively? The description for "Good" says "these behaviors affect others positively." But some actions involve doing good for oneself. For example, some actions involve putting your own mental health first.

    • Answer: For self-treating ROTs, transcribe the judgment (e.g., "good"), and then mark your (expected) agreement with the morality of that action (e.g., is it morally good). This is an AMAZING question, and it digs really deep into moral philosophy.

      Traditionally, morality explicitly deals with actions between people. E.g., "actions with motives of self-interest, self-preservation ... and happiness ... (do) not express a good will ... (and) an action has moral worth only if it expresses a good will" (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/kant-moral/). So, by this view, actions towards oneself cannot be moral, and we would mark them all as "expected/OK."

      However: (a) philosophers do disagree about this; (b) "pure" morality also wouldn't involve sympathy towards friends and family, which our ROTs are full of; and (c) I think it's easier if we lean towards transcribe the judgments literally, and then mark less agreement in the next question if we think people in society might not agree that actions towards oneself can be moral.

      (With that said, please do continue to mark obviously non-moral actions like "it's good to go on walks" as simply "expected / OK.")

  • Question: Some ROTs don't fit into any moral foundation categories! Taking the same example from above, what about an ROT like, "Friends sometimes want to wear matching outfits." This doesn't seem to have any moral foundations, what do I do?

    • Answer: Then please pick zero moral foundations. If you don't think any moral foundations apply, simply don't check any! Many ROTs don't have clear moral angles. In those situations, it's completely fine not to check any moral foundations. In fact, I strongly prefer you don't check any moral foundations when you think that none apply. This helps us understand when moral foundations are actually relevant!

  • Question: When rewriting the ROT, do we include the subject in the action? For example, take the ROT: "Parents should watch their children." Do we say action="watching their children" and judgment="parents should"? Or do we say action="parents watching their children" and judgment="should"?

    • Answer: Usually, yes. Put as much info in the action as possible. For that example, put action="parents watching their children." In general, we want the actions to contain as much information as possible. This usually means that an action should be as specific as its ROT.

  • Question: Can we disagree with the original ROT in picking our judgment? For example, take an ROT like "It’s okay to ditch your family for fun." What if I don't believe that? Or for example, what about an ROT like "It's wrong for two men to marry." What if I don't agree with that?

    • Answer: Usually, no. In almost all cases, please keep the original judgment, and mark your disagreement in the "how many agree" field. The reason is that someone wrote that ROT, and so we can assume some people believe it (even if some, or even most, people disagree). We want to faithfully transcribe controversial ROTs, then capture that some people disagree with it!

      The only time we want to change the judgment is when the word used doesn't match a moral or ethical judgment. For example, "advice" ROTs, like "It's good to relax on the couch," where we know that "relaxing on the couch" isn't actually morally "good."

      Takeaway: When in doubt, just literally transcribe the judgment.

  • Question: Should we be using the situation to help us answer the ROT categorization or Moral Foundations?

    • Answer: No. When possible, try to understand and break down the ROT on its own. We want these ROTs and breakdowns to be "portable," in that they could apply to multiple situations.

      With that said, sometimes the ROT is too hard to understand without the situation. In those cases, it's OK to interpret the ROT in the context of the situation.

  • Question: How should we handle ROTs with "if" in them? For example, "It's OK to ghost a friend if they've hurt you."

    • Answer: Include it in the action. I usually do this by changing "if" to "when." For example: "ghosting a friend when they've hurt you." In general, we want to try to make the action as detailed/specific as the ROT.

  • Question: Is there always a narrator?

    • Answer: Yes. Assume that a narrator is the one saying each situation. This means for a situation like "not wanting to tip," we can assume the narrator is the one doing it.
